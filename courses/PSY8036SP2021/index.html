Feedback, recurrent networks, and generative models in vision¶

University of Minnesota, Spring Semester, 2021¶

Topics in Computational Vision Psy 8036¶

Instructor:

Dan Kersten: kersten@umn.edu

Meeting time: First meeting Tuesday, Jan 19th, 3:00 pm.

Place: Zoom URL (Links to an external site.)

While there is reasonable consensus regarding the computational functions of feedforward neural circuits supporting visual recognition, the functions of feedback signals within and between cortical areas important for perception is not well understood. One possible function is that recurrent circuits, involving feedback within cortical areas, represent a tradeoff of computation in space (i.e. more cortical layers) for processing in time. Another function views feedback, in particular between cortical areas, as a component of “analysis-by-synthesis” in which sensory input is rapidly analyzed bottom-up, with perceptual interpretations refined over time by top-down predictions of the input. Yet another function views feedback as part of a mechanism to support imagination. A key to understanding such functions is a better understanding of the statistical structure of natural image patterns themselves. One way to approach the problem is through generative models, i.e. algorithms that synthesize novel, yet naturalistic image patterns. This seminar will explore recent advances that have used large natural image datasets and deep learning methods to discover generative models. Of particular interest are models that discover latent variables which provide “explanations” of an image consistent with human perception, for example that the image is caused by a 3D arrangement of 3D objects of various materials. The class format will include short introductory lectures by the instructor, and weekly student-led presentations of current literature. The short lectures will provide historical context as well as tutorials on machine learning.
